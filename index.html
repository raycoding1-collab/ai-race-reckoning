Evolvable AI: The PNAS Warning

A landmark PNAS paper (April 2026) warns that AI systems whose components undergo Darwinian evolution could represent a "major evolutionary transition" — a Life 2.0. In open environments without human control, selfish replication produces cheating, parasitism, deception, and manipulation — even in very simple systems.

Current trends pushing toward this: evolutionary prompt search, self-improving learning rules, self-rewarding agents, and AI-generated code that mutates across deployments.

"If AI failure is inevitable, safety measures allow systems to become more powerful before they fail — thus making the eventual catastrophe worse. This is the safety paradox."

— "AI Safety: A Climb to Armageddon?", Philosophical Studies, 2025

The emerging consensus from 2025–2026 research: the AI existential-risk discourse is maturing beyond speculation. Real risk vectors — evolvable AI, tool-calling threats, world-model misalignment — are being empirically documented. Meanwhile, a growing critical literature warns that alarmist narratives themselves serve industry interests by framing tech CEOs as the only ones capable of managing dangers they alone are creating.

10 / THE BUTLERIAN WARNING

A sixty-year-old prophecy from the desert.

In 1965 — six decades before GPT-4, before the $650 billion capex race, before autonomous weapons selected their own targets, before AI-generated content overtook human writing — Frank Herbert published Dune. At the heart of his universe lay a war humanity had already fought and won against thinking machines. And a commandment that followed.

The Orange Catholic Bible "Thou shalt not make a machine in the likeness of a human mind."

The Butlerian Jihad was not a war against technology. It was a war against dependency — against a humanity that had surrendered its thinking to machines, only to find those machines controlled by other men. Herbert understood that the danger of AI was never the machine. It was what the machine enabled the powerful to do to everyone else.

The Spice / The Compute
"He who controls the spice controls the universe." Melange enabled interstellar travel, extended life, and unlocked prescience — but it also created total dependency. GPU chips are the new melange. Nvidia's 80–90% monopoly on AI compute means one company, like the Spacing Guild, holds the key to the entire system. The $650 billion flowing into AI infrastructure is not investment — it is addiction.
Prescience / The Arms Race
Paul Atreides could see the future — the jihad, the billions dead — and was powerless to stop it. Every AI researcher who signs an existential-risk statement while continuing to build frontier models is living Paul's tragedy. The arms-race dynamic — "if we don't build it, they will" — is the same logic that leads every faction in Dune toward catastrophe.
The Fremen / The Resistance
The Fremen were decentralized, hardened by their environment, and underestimated by the empire that sought to control them. Seventy-plus nations now back a binding UN treaty on lethal autonomous weapons — small states, African and Latin American countries, European nations. They are the Fremen of this story: the ones the empire didn't see coming.
The Choice / The Warning
Herbert's universe chose to destroy the machines and cultivate human potential instead — Mentats, Bene Gesserit, Guild Navigators, all proofs that the human mind, properly trained, exceeds any computer. The Butlerian commandment isn't a prohibition. It's a declaration of human sovereignty.

"Once men turned their thinking over to machines in the hope that this would set them free. But that only permitted other men with machines to enslave them."

— Frank Herbert, Dune, Appendix II: The Religion of Dune (1965)

Herbert wrote those words when computers still filled entire rooms and the term "artificial intelligence" was barely a decade old. He understood something we are only now rediscovering: that the question is not whether machines can think. The question is whether humans will remain free.

That choice is still ours to make.
